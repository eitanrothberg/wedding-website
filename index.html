It wasn't long after first meeting in college that Eitan and Marili liked each other. Okay, liked liked. But still:

Eitan, marry a Long Island princess who doesn't play board games? Could Marili's true love be a suburban Ohioan who spends his Saturdays debating bizarre hypotheticals?

Yes indeed! Through a pandemic, through a cross-country move, and through years of short-, medium-, and long-distance dating, their relationship has steadily been their greatest joy and adventure.

Marili opened Eitan's eyes to the wonders of carne asada and folded laundry, and Eitan shared with Marili the magic of Cape Cod and Arrested Development. And together, they discovered some of their new favorite things: tropical vacations. Saturday night pizza and sitcoms. Farmers' markets with live music.

Eitan and Marili, Marili and Eitan. These BFsF, these strange and happy lovebirds.

Traditional Jewish Weddings

We are thrilled you are here to help us mark this special moment in our lives.
According to Jewish tradition, a wedding is not just about two people finding happiness, it is a moment of celebration for all assembled.

Kippot

It is customary that Jewish males wear kippot (skullcaps) as a head covering. It demonstrates the awareness that there is something which is infinitely above our intellect and symbolizes our respect and humility. Non- Jewish males may choose to wear a kippah during the ceremony as well.

Kebutah

The tradition of the ketubah (marriage contract) is at least two thousand years old. Two witnesses sign the ketubah, which expresses our obligations to each other and our commitment to establish a Jewish home.

Chuppah

The ceremony takes place under a chuppah (wedding canopy). It represents the home we are creating together, and it is open on all sides to welcome all who enter. As a fragile temporary structure, the chuppah reminds us of the fragility of life and that the most important thing about a home is not its physical construction but the people in and around it.

Kittel

When Noah arrives under the Chuppah his parents will help him put on a white linen garment known as a kittel. The pristine white kittel is traditionally worn on Yom Kippur, a symbol of atonement and total purity. The white clothing of the bride and groom are a metaphor for the type of relationship they are now entering. Both bride and groom bring a kaleidoscope of colors into the marriage; some bright and some murky. All these colors are embedded upon a "white" canvas, the pure essence of the soul. The white clothing reflects the commitment of the couple to establish a deep connection which touches at their very core.

Hakafot

When Chaya arrives at the chuppah she will circle Noah seven times. While there are many explanations for this ritual, we love the suggestion that marriage marks the creation of our new life together and the seven circles symbolize seven rotations of the earth – for the seven days of creation.

Sheva Brachot

The real heart of the Jewish wedding ceremony is the Sheva Brachot (seven Blessings). The blessings begin by celebrating God’s role as the creator of all, and evoke images of humanity, parenthood, friendship, and love – at last focusing on the bride and groom themselves. The final blessing offers ten synonyms for the word joy.

Breaking the Glass

Noah will break a glass. The shattered glass symbolizes the fragility of relationships, of happiness, and of human life itself. It also reminds us that suffering continues to exist – and that, just as we began the ceremony by invoking God’s act of creation, we end with an acknowledgement that the work of creating a more whole world is ours to complete. Amidst music, singing and shouts of Mazel Tov, the party begins.
