- Marili Ressel. Letter to Eitan. 2020.

It wasn't long after first meeting in college that Eitan and Marili liked each other. Okay, liked liked. But still:

Eitan, marry a chatty Long Islander who's more interested in fashion than board games? And could Marili's true love really be a suburban Ohioan with a fierce vendetta against creamy salads?

Yes indeed! Through a pandemic, through a cross-country move, and through years of short-, medium-, and long-distance dating, their relationship has steadily been their greatest joy and adventure.

Marili opened Eitan's eyes to the wonders of carne asada and folded laundry, and Eitan shared with Marili the magic of Cape Cod and Middle Earth. And together, they discovered some of their new favorite things, from tropical vacations and Saturday night pizza to road trips and farmers markets with live music.

What does the future hold for these best of friends, these strange and happy lovebirds?

Only time will tell. For now, they are focused on September 3rd, on their beloved friends and family, and on just how sweet everything seems to taste.

Traditional Jewish Weddings

We are thrilled you are here to help us mark this special moment in our lives.
According to Jewish tradition, a wedding is not just about two people finding happiness, it is a moment of celebration for all assembled.

Kippot

It is customary that Jewish males wear kippot (skullcaps) as a head covering. They represent our respect and humility in the presence of God, infinitely above our intellect and understanding. Non-Jewish males may choose to wear a kippah during the ceremony as well.

Kabalat Panim

Before the marriage ceremony begins, guests are invited into the kabbalat panim, the initial receptions held for the groom and bride. Men will join Eitan at his tisch - the groom’s reception - and women will remain with Marili at the bridal reception.

Ketubah

At the tisch, the ketubah (marriage contract) will be formally signed by two witnesses. This tradition, at least two thousand years old, establishes the couple's obligations to each other and their commitment to establish a Jewish home.

Following the signing, Eitan’s mother and Marili’s mother will break a plate together, representing the importance of Eitan and Marili’s commitment to one another. Friends and family will then dance Eitan out of the room and towards the bridal reception for the bedeken.

Bedeken

The bedeken is a moment of great excitement as it is the first time in a week that Eitan and Marili will see each other. Eitan will place a veil over Marili’s face out of respect for her modesty and inner beauty. Then everyone will make their way to the chuppah for the official marriage ceremony.

Chuppah

The marriage ceremony takes place under a chuppah (wedding canopy), whose open walls symbolize unconditional hospitality and the home that Eitan and Marili will build together. As a fragile and temporary structure, the chuppah reminds us of the impermanence of life and that the most important thing about a home is not its physical construction, but the people in and around it.

Hakafot

When Marili arrives at the chuppah, she will circle Eitan seven times, an allusion to the seven days of creation and the new world they will build together.

Sheva Brachot

Hakafot are followed by the heart of the Jewish wedding ceremony: the sheva brachot, the seven blessings celebrating God’s role as the creator of all. These blessings, bestowed by dear friends and family, evoke images of humanity, parenthood, friendship and love – at last focusing on the bride and groom themselves. The final blessing offers ten synonyms for the word joy.

Breaking the Glass

Eitan will break a glass. The shattered glass symbolizes the fragility of relationships, of happiness, and of human life itself. It also reminds us that suffering continues to exist. Just as we began the ceremony by invoking God’s act of creation, we end by acknowleding our role in making that world whole.
Amidst music, singing, and shouts of Mazel Tov, the party begins.
